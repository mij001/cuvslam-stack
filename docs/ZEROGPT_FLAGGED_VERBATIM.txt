\section{Motivation: Physical AI Meets the Memory Wall}


[2] chapters/ch1_introduction.tex:5
------------------------------------------------------------------
A robot navigating the world must continuously, and in real time, answer two questions: \emph{Where am I?} and \emph{What does my surrounding look like?} The computational discipline that answers both from camera images is visual Simultaneous Localization and Mapping (SLAM). In production robotics, SLAM workloads typically run on GPUs. This thesis studies cuVSLAM~\cite{cuvslam2025}, NVIDIA's CUDA-accelerated visual SLAM library, which is shipped inside the Isaac robotics stack and deployed on real robots. By construction, this workload is representative of what is now called \emph{Physical AI}: machines that sense and act in the physical world under strict latency and energy constraints.


[3] chapters/ch1_introduction.tex:7 (only the middle sentence was flagged, shown in context)
------------------------------------------------------------------
For such machines, the primary processor constraint is no longer arithmetic. Decades of technological advancement have made floating-point operations nearly free compared to the cost of \emph{moving the operands}: fetching a word from off-chip DRAM consumes orders of magnitude more energy than computation, and this gap widens with each generation. This phenomenon is known as the \emph{memory wall}~\cite{wulf1995memorywall}, the modern manifestation of the von Neumann bottleneck. For battery-powered robots, the consequence is direct: joules spent moving pixels and map entries are joules unavailable for flight time or actuation.


[4] chapters/ch2_background.tex:8
------------------------------------------------------------------
A CPU optimizes the latency of a single instruction stream; a GPU is designed to maximize the throughput of tens of thousands. NVIDIA GPUs are programmed using CUDA: the programmer writes a \emph{kernel} a single function and launches it over a grid of thousands to millions of \emph{threads}, each processing a distinct data element. Threads are grouped in fixed bundles of 32, called \emph{warps}; the 32 \emph{lanes} of a warp execute the same instruction in lockstep on different data. Warps are grouped into thread blocks, and blocks are scheduled onto \emph{streaming multiprocessors} (SMs) the GPU's core unit. The primary instrument used in this thesis, an NVIDIA RTX~2000 Ada generation GPU (compute capability \code{sm\_89}), features 22~SMs, a 25\,MB shared L2 cache, and 16\,GB of GDDR6 DRAM.


[5] chapters/ch2_background.tex:16
------------------------------------------------------------------
\emph{Occupancy} is the fraction of an SM's maximum concurrent warps that are resident. It is the GPU's latency-hiding mechanism: while one warp waits for memory, the scheduler issues another. High occupancy can hide DRAM \emph{latency} completely; however, nothing can hide a \emph{bandwidth} limitation. This asymmetry latency can be hidden, bandwidth cannot shapes the entire bottleneck taxonomy discussed in Chapter~\ref{ch:method}.


[6] chapters/ch2_background.tex:89
------------------------------------------------------------------
cuVSLAM~\cite{cuvslam2025} is NVIDIA's production implementation: GPU-resident front-end and solvers; a host-side keyframe/landmark store backed by LMDB (a memory-mapped embedded database); and support for stereo, RGB-D (color plus depth), monocular, and inertial (IMU-fused) camera configurations, in odometry-only or full-SLAM modes, with both synchronous and asynchronous SLAM variants. This thesis treats cuVSLAM strictly as a measured artifact: its algorithms remain unmodified; instrumentation is additive and switchable, and Chapter~\ref{ch:validity} proves its output-neutrality.


[7] chapters/ch2_background.tex:91
------------------------------------------------------------------
SLAM system accuracy is evaluated against \emph{ground truth} (the true trajectory from motion capture, GPS/INS, or an external tracker), using two standard metrics~\cite{sturm2012tum,zhang2018tutorial}: \emph{absolute pose/trajectory error} (APE/ATE) the RMS position difference after optimal alignment (SE(3) for metric-scale systems; Sim(3) when monocular scale is unconstrained~\cite{umeyama1991}) and \emph{relative pose/trajectory error} (RPE/RTE) drift over fixed-length segments, expressed as a percentage. The latter is scale-independent and thus comparable between kilometre-scale (KITTI) and room-scale (EuRoC, TUM) trajectories.
