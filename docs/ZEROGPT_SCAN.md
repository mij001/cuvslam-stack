### Chapter 1 — Introduction — 25.1%

- Motivation: Physical AI Meets the Memory Wall
- A robot navigating the world must continuously, and in real time, answer two questions: Where am I? and What does my surrounding look like?
- The computational discipline that answers both from camera images is visual Simultaneous Localization and Mapping (SLAM).
- In production robotics, SLAM workloads typically run on GPUs.
- This thesis studies cuVSLAM, NVIDIA's CUDA-accelerated visual SLAM library, which is shipped inside the Isaac robotics stack and deployed on real robots.
- By construction, this workload is representative of what is now called Physical AI: machines that sense and act in the physical world under strict latency and energy constraints.
- This phenomenon is known as the memory wall, the modern manifestation of the von Neumann bottleneck.

### Chapter 2 — Background — 10.7%

- A CPU optimizes the latency of a single instruction stream; a GPU is designed to maximize the throughput of tens of thousands.
- NVIDIA GPUs are programmed using CUDA: the programmer writes a kernel, a single function, and launches it over a grid of thousands to millions of threads, each processing a distinct data element.
- Threads are grouped in fixed bundles of 32, called warps; the 32 lanes of a warp execute the same instruction in lockstep on different data.
- It is the GPU's latency-hiding mechanism: while one warp waits for memory, the scheduler issues another.
- High occupancy can hide DRAM latency completely; however, nothing can hide a bandwidth limitation.
- This thesis treats cuVSLAM strictly as a measured artifact: its algorithms remain unmodified, instrumentation is additive and switchable, and later chapters prove its output-neutrality.
- SLAM system accuracy is evaluated against ground truth, the true trajectory from motion capture, GPS/INS, or an external tracker, using two standard metrics: absolute pose or trajectory error, the RMS position difference after optimal alignment, and relative pose or trajectory error, drift over fixed-length segments expressed as a percentage.

### Chapter 4 — Methodology — 6.2%

- The primary instrument is an NVIDIA RTX 2000 Ada GPU: 22 SMs, 100 KB unified L1/shared per SM, 25 MB L2, 16 GB GDDR6.
- A second, deliberately different microarchitecture, a GeForce MX450 with 2 GB and a 25 W budget, is used only for cross-device validation.
- The host workstation runs driver 575.64.05 with CUDA 12.9.1; profilers are Nsight Compute 2025.2.1.3 and Nsight Systems 2025.3.2; address tracing uses NVBit 1.8.

- Memory intensity is measured in DRAM 32-byte-sector fetches per thousand warp instructions.
- The warp-level denominator is thirty-two times smaller than the thread-level one; conflating them is a classic factor-of-32 error.

## Method

1. Each chapter's `.tex` source was stripped to plain prose: LaTeX commands unwrapped (`\textbf{x}` → `x`), figures/tables/tikz/math environments removed, `\cite`/`\ref`/`\label` dropped, comments stripped.
2. A few chunks needed manual reconstruction where the stripper had removed content that depended on an equation or a stripped list (chapters 1, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11) — reconstructed to preserve the original meaning and all figures, never rewritten for style.
3. Each chapter (2,600–9,400 characters) was pasted into ZeroGPT's input box individually and submitted via "Detect Text"; free-tier limit is 15,000 characters, so no chapter needed splitting.
4. The percentage, verdict, and highlighted (`<mark>`) sentences were read directly from the page's DOM after each submission.

## Caveat

AI-detection tools including ZeroGPT have well-documented false-positive rates on formal/technical writing — dense terminology, consistent structure, and definitional sentences ("X is Y: A, B, and C") read as "AI-like" to these classifiers regardless of authorship. Every flagged sentence above is a definitional or specification-listing sentence (hardware specs, dataset enumerations, tool-version lists); none of the flagged material is a finding, a result, or a novel claim — those chapters and passages scored 0% throughout. This scan is one non-authoritative tool's opinion on one pass, not evidence of AI authorship or its absence.
